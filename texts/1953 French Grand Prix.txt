<p>The <b>1953 French Grand Prix</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Formula_Two" title="Formula Two">Formula Two</a> race held on 5 July 1953 at <a href="/wiki/Reims-Gueux" title="Reims-Gueux">Reims</a>. It was race 5 of 9 in the <a href="/wiki/1953_World_Championship_of_Drivers" class="mw-redirect" title="1953 World Championship of Drivers">1953 World Championship of Drivers</a>, which was run to Formula Two rules in 1952 and 1953, rather than the Formula One regulations normally used.
</p><p>It is popularly known as <b>The Race of the Century</b> because of the sixty lap battle between Briton Mike Hawthorn and Argentine Juan Manuel Fangio.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Hawthorn won the duel after they reportedly swapped the lead at virtually every corner on the Reims circuit. In addition, after 500&#160;km of racing, the four lead cars were less than 5 seconds apart.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1953_French_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For 1953, the Reims circuit’s layout changed. The new, faster and slightly longer circuit bypassed the town of Gueux and as a result, the circuit was now called just “Reims”.
</p><p>Coming into the French Grand Prix, <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Ferrari</a> driver, and <a href="/wiki/1952_World_Championship_of_Drivers" class="mw-redirect" title="1952 World Championship of Drivers">1952</a> World Champion <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Ascari" title="Alberto Ascari">Alberto Ascari</a> had a large lead in the championship, having won the first three races of the season (not including the <a href="/wiki/1953_Indianapolis_500" title="1953 Indianapolis 500">Indianapolis 500</a> in which none of the Grand Prix contenders took part). Meanwhile early favourite, <a href="/wiki/1951_World_Championship_of_Drivers" class="mw-redirect" title="1951 World Championship of Drivers">1951</a> World Champion <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel_Fangio" title="Juan Manuel Fangio">Juan Manuel Fangio</a>, driving for <a href="/wiki/Maserati_in_motorsport" title="Maserati in motorsport">Maserati</a>, had yet to even finish a World Championship qualifying event in 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Grant_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grant-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike in 1952, in 1953 Maserati and Ferrari were quite evenly matched. The Maseratis had slightly more power, and hence straight-line speed, but the Ferraris had slightly better brakes, road holding, and low-end acceleration. Both works teams sent four drivers. For Ferrari there was Ascari, <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Villoresi" title="Luigi Villoresi">Luigi Villoresi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Farina" title="Giuseppe Farina">Nino Farina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mike_Hawthorn" title="Mike Hawthorn">Mike Hawthorn</a>. Maserati had Fangio, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Froil%C3%A1n_Gonz%C3%A1lez" title="José Froilán González">José Froilán González</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onofre_Marim%C3%B3n" title="Onofre Marimón">Onofre Marimón</a> and <a href="/wiki/Felice_Bonetto" title="Felice Bonetto">Felice Bonetto</a>. Both Ferrari and Maserati were also represented by a single private entry each, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Rosier" title="Louis Rosier">Louis Rosier</a> driving a Ferrari and <a href="/wiki/Toulo_de_Graffenried" title="Toulo de Graffenried">Toulo de Graffenried</a> a Maserati.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All of the other entries had little chance of winning, as they had significantly less power than the Ferraris and Maseratis, as well as generally worse road holding. They consisted of two privately entered <a href="/wiki/O.S.C.A." title="O.S.C.A.">OSCAs</a> (one to be driven by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Chiron" title="Louis Chiron">Louis Chiron</a> in his last French Grand Prix start), four works <a href="/wiki/Gordini" title="Gordini">Gordinis</a> and three of each of <a href="/wiki/Connaught_Engineering" title="Connaught Engineering">Connaught</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hersham_and_Walton_Motors" title="Hersham and Walton Motors">HWM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Car_Company" title="Cooper Car Company">Cooper</a>. The works Gordinis were poorly prepared, the team instead focusing on the <a href="/wiki/12_Hours_of_Reims" title="12 Hours of Reims">12 hour race</a> which ran from midnight to midday on the same day as the Grand Prix. Two of the Connaughts were works entries, and were notable as the first <a href="/wiki/Fuel_injection" title="Fuel injection">fuel injected</a> cars to start the French Grand Prix.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Practice.2FQualifying"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Practice/Qualifying">Practice/Qualifying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1953_French_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Practice/Qualifying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There was very little activity in the earlier practice sessions, with the Ferrari team only arriving just in time for the final session. González was the early pacesetter for Maserati, but was eventually outdone by both Villoresi and Ascari for Ferrari. González would again set the fastest time of 2:41.5, but in Bonetto’s car, but Ascari would finally take pole position with a time of 2:41.2 late in the session. The front row of the 3-2-3 grid was therefore Ascari, Bonetto and Villoresi, with Fangio and González sharing row two.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lang_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The fastest six cars were separated by just 1.3 seconds, with the Ferraris and Maseratis clearly quite evenly matched. The first non-Ferrari or Maserati was the Connaught of <a href="/wiki/Birabongse_Bhanudej" title="Birabongse Bhanudej">Prince Bira</a> with 2:53.2, 12 seconds slower than Ascari and almost 4 seconds slower than Rosier in the slowest Ferrari.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lang_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-race_controversy">Pre-race controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1953_French_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pre-race controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Grand Prix was preceded by the 12 hour sportscar race which ran from midnight to midday. During the race, the leading Ferrari, driven by <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Maglioli" title="Umberto Maglioli">Umberto Maglioli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piero_Carini" title="Piero Carini">Piero Carini</a>, was disqualified, ostensibly for receiving a push start, and for switching off sidelights before the appointed time. Many, including Ferrari team manager Ugolini, felt this quite unfair, since the push-start had been to get clear of spilt petrol in the pitlane, and nearly every other car in the race had already switched off their sidelights by the time Maglioli did the same. Many in the crowd also disagreed with the disqualification, with the crowd booing and throwing rubbish at head officials <a href="/wiki/Charles_Faroux" title="Charles Faroux">Charles Faroux</a> and Toto Roche.<sup id="cite_ref-Grant_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grant-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a result, Ferrari threatened to withdraw their cars, which would have surely handed Maserati an easy win. However after several phone calls between Reims and Modena, the Ferraris were eventually allowed to start in the Grand Prix.<sup id="cite_ref-Grant_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grant-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lang_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Race">Race</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1953_French_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Race">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the race, González decided to start with half a tank of fuel and make a pitstop in the race, while all of the other main contenders started with full tanks hoping to last the whole distance. This of course meant that González would need to build a large enough gap to make his pitstop.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the start, from the front row Bonetto and Ascari both made good starts, while Villoresi was slow away, leaving a gap for González to quickly move into the lead with his much lighter car. At the end of the first lap González had built a 2.8 second gap over the other Italian cars, the order being Ascari, Villoresi, Bonetto, Fangio, Hawthorn, Farina and Marimón, with the gap from second to eighth just 2.2 seconds. Further back was the first of the non-Italian cars, with Bira just ahead of the Gordini of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Trintignant" title="Maurice Trintignant">Maurice Trintignant</a>, who had started from the back having not set a lap in practice. <sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the second lap, González continued to pull away from the main pack, now led by the Ferraris of Ascari, Villoresi and Hawthorn, all disputing second place, with Farina close behind. They were followed by the Maseratis of Fangio and Marimón, with Bonetto dropping to ninth after a spin, behind Trintignant, Bira and de Graffenried. Apart from the three Ferraris contesting second place, the order near the front remained the same for the next 20 laps or so, at which point González ceased increasing his lead, making it unlikely he would be able to hold it when he made his stop.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On lap 23, Fangio overtook Farina, who responded by setting the then fastest lap of the race and retaking the position. On the following lap Fangio overtook Farina again, setting the fastest lap of the race in the process. This increase in pace of Fangio and Farina had now placed them in the middle of the three Ferraris, Fangio now in third place. Some shuffling of the pack took place by the time González made his pitstop on lap 29, with Fangio now leading it ahead of Hawthorn, with Villoresi dropping back to Marimón. González’s pitstop took just 27 seconds, but this allowed Fangio into the lead, with González dropping all the way down to sixth, ahead of Villoresi but behind Marimón who had just passed him.<sup id="cite_ref-Lang_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At half distance, Fangio lead Hawthorn and Ascari, the top three separated by less than a second, followed at small intervals by Farina, Marimón, then González and Villoresi just a second apart, around 20 seconds behind Fangio. Bonetto was over a minute and 20 seconds behind Fangio, and no other drivers were still on the lead lap.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Over the next few laps, Fangio and Hawthorn would swap the lead several times, sometimes more than once a lap, pulling slowly away from Ascari who was locked in a close battle with Farina, González and Marimón. Villoresi, meanwhile, fell back, but not enough to be challenged for seventh place. González continued to push, catching and overtaking Farina then Ascari on lap 37. This spurred Ascari on, and he and González duelled for third place over the following 20 laps. Both the duels, for first place and for third place, would last until very near the end of the race, with the drivers separated by not more than a carlength at any stage.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With two laps to go, Fangio and Hawthorn crossed the finish line side by side, followed less than a second later by González and Ascari, also side by side. Hawthorn led into the last lap, with González very close now, but Ascari well off the pace having eased off. Coming into the final straight González was able to overlap slightly on Fangio, but Hawthorn lead the pair, winning the race by just one second from Fangio, with González just 0.4 seconds behind in third place.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodges_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodges-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
