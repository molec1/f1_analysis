<p>The <b>1971 Italian Grand Prix</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> motor race held at <a href="/wiki/Autodromo_Nazionale_di_Monza" title="Autodromo Nazionale di Monza">Monza</a> on 5 September 1971. It was race 9 of 11 in both the <a href="/wiki/1971_World_Championship_of_Drivers" class="mw-redirect" title="1971 World Championship of Drivers">1971 World Championship of Drivers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1971_International_Cup_for_Formula_One_Manufacturers" class="mw-redirect" title="1971 International Cup for Formula One Manufacturers">1971 International Cup for Formula One Manufacturers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This race featured the closest finish in Formula One history, as <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gethin" title="Peter Gethin">Peter Gethin</a> beat <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Peterson" title="Ronnie Peterson">Ronnie Peterson</a> by 0.01 seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-closest_finish_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-closest_finish-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The top five were covered by just 0.61 seconds, with <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Cevert" title="François Cevert">François Cevert</a> finishing third, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Hailwood" title="Mike Hailwood">Mike Hailwood</a> fourth and <a href="/wiki/Howden_Ganley" title="Howden Ganley">Howden Ganley</a> fifth. With an average speed of 242.615&#160;km/h (150.754&#160;mph), this race stood as the fastest-ever Formula One race for 32 years, until the <a href="/wiki/2003_Italian_Grand_Prix" title="2003 Italian Grand Prix">2003 Italian Grand Prix</a> at Monza.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Race_report">Race report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1971_Italian_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Race report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The historical Monza National Autodrome, located just north of the northern Italian city of Milan, in 1971 became the fastest circuit used by Formula One after the Belgian <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_Spa-Francorchamps" title="Circuit de Spa-Francorchamps">Spa-Francorchamps circuit</a> was removed from the calendar. However, this was the last year in which the circuit was used with this configuration: considering the enormous speed that the cars reached in this edition, two chicanes were introduced the following year in the two most dangerous curves of the track.
</p><p>With the championship settled, this was an opportunity for new drivers to prove themselves. <a href="/wiki/Chris_Amon" title="Chris Amon">Chris Amon</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Equipe_Matra_Sports" title="Equipe Matra Sports">Matra</a> proved an embarrassment to Ferrari by seizing pole at their home track with the fastest lap of all time in a Formula One championship race, lapping at 156 mph (252 km/h), with the BRMs on the second row, whilst champion Stewart was in 6th after suffering gearbox problems. <a href="/wiki/Mike_Hailwood" title="Mike Hailwood">Mike Hailwood</a> was making his debut for <a href="/wiki/Surtees" title="Surtees">Surtees</a>—an inspired choice as he held both the Formula 5000 and motorbike lap records for Monza.
<a href="/wiki/Clay_Regazzoni" title="Clay Regazzoni">Clay Regazzoni</a>'s Ferrari thrilled the crowd by surging forward from the fourth row to lead from <a href="/wiki/Jo_Siffert" title="Jo Siffert">Jo Siffert</a> and Stewart until lap 3, when <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Peterson" title="Ronnie Peterson">Ronnie Peterson</a> took the lead. On lap 7, Stewart took the lead. By lap 16, Stewart and <a href="/wiki/Jacky_Ickx" title="Jacky Ickx">Jacky Ickx</a> retired with engine problems, followed two laps later by Clay Regazzoni. The race began to break into high-speed packs—the leading one containing Hailwood (leading on his debut), <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Cevert" title="François Cevert">François Cevert</a>, Peterson, Siffert, <a href="/wiki/Howden_Ganley" title="Howden Ganley">Howden Ganley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Amon" title="Chris Amon">Chris Amon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gethin" title="Peter Gethin">Peter Gethin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Oliver" title="Jackie Oliver">Jackie Oliver</a>. Gethin, Peterson, Cevert, Hailwood and Ganley (who fell back slightly) battled right down to the line and all finished within two-tenths of a second of each other.
Siffert dropped back after problems with a gearbox that would only select fourth gear.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1971_Italian_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Emerson_Fittipaldi" title="Emerson Fittipaldi">Emerson Fittipaldi</a> drove a <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel_drive" title="Four-wheel drive">four-wheel drive</a> <a href="/wiki/Lotus_56B" class="mw-redirect" title="Lotus 56B">Lotus 56B</a> powered by a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a>, the only time he would race in a Formula One World Championship race in a car not powered by a <a href="/wiki/Ford_Cosworth" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford Cosworth">Ford Cosworth</a> <a href="/wiki/Ford_Cosworth_DFV" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford Cosworth DFV">DFV</a> engine. Due to ongoing legal issues between Team Lotus and the Italian authorities following <a href="/wiki/Jochen_Rindt" title="Jochen Rindt">Jochen Rindt</a>'s death the <a href="/wiki/1970_Italian_Grand_Prix" title="1970 Italian Grand Prix">previous year</a>, the car was entered under the name "World Wide Racing".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Tyrrell-Ford won their first Constructors' Championship with two races remaining.</li>
<li>This was the last race ever to be held on the extremely fast, chicane-less Monza circuit. Chicanes made of tyre walls were installed for the <a href="/wiki/1972_Italian_Grand_Prix" title="1972 Italian Grand Prix">following year's race</a>.</li>
<li>This was the fastest race lap at 242.615 km/h (150.754 mph); a record which stood for 32 years until the <a href="/wiki/2003_Italian_Grand_Prix" title="2003 Italian Grand Prix">2003 event</a>.</li>
<li>This was the closest finish in Formula One history, as <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gethin" title="Peter Gethin">Peter Gethin</a> beat <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Peterson" title="Ronnie Peterson">Ronnie Peterson</a> by 0.01 seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-closest_finish_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-closest_finish-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>This was the only win for <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gethin" title="Peter Gethin">Peter Gethin</a>.</li>
<li>This was also the final win for an <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> driver until <a href="/wiki/James_Hunt" title="James Hunt">James Hunt</a> won the <a href="/wiki/1975_Dutch_Grand_Prix" title="1975 Dutch Grand Prix">1975 Dutch Grand Prix</a>.</li></ul>
