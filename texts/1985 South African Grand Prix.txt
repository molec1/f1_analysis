<p>The <b>1985 South African Grand Prix</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> <a href="/wiki/Motor_race" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor race">motor race</a> held on 19 October 1985 at the <a href="/wiki/Kyalami" title="Kyalami">Kyalami Circuit</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. It was the fifteenth and penultimate round of the <a href="/wiki/1985_FIA_Formula_One_World_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="1985 FIA Formula One World Championship">1985 FIA Formula One World Championship</a>, the last World Championship Grand Prix to be held on a Saturday and the last World Championship Grand Prix where laurel wreaths were given to the drivers at the podium.
</p><p>The race was marked with some teams boycotting the event due to <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> – the segregation of <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">blacks</a> and <a href="/wiki/White_South_Africans" title="White South Africans">whites</a> – and was the last South African Formula One race until apartheid ended in <a href="/wiki/1992_South_African_Grand_Prix" title="1992 South African Grand Prix">1992</a>. The race was won by <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Williams_FW10" title="Williams FW10">Williams</a>-<a href="/wiki/Honda_in_Formula_One" title="Honda in Formula One">Honda</a>, who also took <a href="/wiki/Pole_position" title="Pole position">pole position</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Report">Report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1985_South_African_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The event was boycotted by two teams, <a href="/wiki/Equipe_Ligier" title="Equipe Ligier">Ligier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renault_in_Formula_One" title="Renault in Formula One">Renault</a>, owing to <a href="/wiki/Sporting_boycott_of_South_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Sporting boycott of South Africa">mounting international pressures</a> against tolerating the country's system of apartheid. <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> teams Ligier and Renault's boycotts were in lockstep with the <a href="/wiki/French_government" class="mw-redirect" title="French government">French government</a>'s boycott and sanctioning of South Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> apparently doing so under pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-norace86_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-norace86-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Most of the Formula One drivers, including <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niki_Lauda" title="Niki Lauda">Niki Lauda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a> were personally very much against racing in South Africa, but the drivers held the mentality that because they were contracted to drive at every Grand Prix, they would race at Kyalami. <sup id="cite_ref-f1fanatic_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-f1fanatic-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Some governments tried to keep their drivers from entering the race. Brazil's sanctions on South Africa nearly prevented <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Piquet" title="Nelson Piquet">Nelson Piquet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a> from racing.<sup id="cite_ref-sfchronic_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sfchronic-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Finland and Sweden held similar reservations regarding Finn <a href="/wiki/Keke_Rosberg" title="Keke Rosberg">Keke Rosberg</a> and Swede <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Johansson" title="Stefan Johansson">Stefan Johansson</a> competing.<sup id="cite_ref-sfchronic_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sfchronic-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Sweden's National Automobile Federation had announced Johansson could not race in South Africa before the event,<sup id="cite_ref-sfchronic_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sfchronic-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but he did race.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a> changed his mind on personally boycotting the race, saying he would race if his Lotus team went.<sup id="cite_ref-senna_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-senna-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It did and he did, retiring outside the points.
</p><p>Multiple sponsors also ordered teams to remove their branding from cars they backed, most notably <a href="/wiki/Marlboro_(cigarette)" title="Marlboro (cigarette)">Marlboro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Foods" title="Beatrice Foods">Beatrice Foods</a>.
</p><p>The latter held an equity interest in the single car <a href="/wiki/Haas_Lola" title="Haas Lola">Haas Lola</a> team. While <a href="/wiki/Alan_Jones_(racing_driver)" title="Alan Jones (racing driver)">Alan Jones</a> qualified 18th for that team, his car was not on the starting grid. Officially Jones cited illness as to why he did not race, but it was widely rumored at the time that Beatrice ordered the team to boycott.<sup id="cite_ref-f1fanatic_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-f1fanatic-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 2017, Jones described a meeting with <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Ecclestone" title="Bernie Ecclestone">Bernie Ecclestone</a> the night before the race, who suggested that Jones feign illness the next morning and not show up. Ecclestone described how Beatrice were under pressure in the US from activists such as <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Jackson" title="Jesse Jackson">Jesse Jackson</a> not to race, under threats including strike action by African Americans working in their businesses. Only Jones and team management <a href="/wiki/Teddy_Mayer" title="Teddy Mayer">Teddy Mayer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Haas" title="Carl Haas">Carl Haas</a> were aware of this plan. Jones said "And so, on the Saturday morning I was gone. I just didn’t turn up. They had the car out ready to go, when they were told, “AJ’s been struck down by a virus and we are not racing.”"<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was the final South African Grand Prix until apartheid ended, with <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_du_Sport_Automobile" title="Fédération Internationale du Sport Automobile">FISA</a> president <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marie_Balestre" title="Jean-Marie Balestre">Jean-Marie Balestre</a> announcing days after the race that a Grand Prix would not return to the nation because of apartheid.<sup id="cite_ref-norace86_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-norace86-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Even without the political pressures, this might well have been the final Formula One race held at Kyalami in its then form: FISA had long since deemed that circuits where lap times were under 60 seconds were considered too small for Grand Prix racing and with car speeds increasing all the time, it was reasonable to conclude that lap times from <a href="/wiki/1986_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="1986 Formula One World Championship">1986</a> would be under 60 seconds. Kyalami's pole position time had actually fallen by over 10 seconds since the <a href="/wiki/1981_South_African_Grand_Prix" title="1981 South African Grand Prix">1981 race</a>, and Mansell's 1985 pole time of 1:02.366 was over two seconds faster than Nelson Piquet's <a href="/wiki/1984_South_African_Grand_Prix" title="1984 South African Grand Prix">1984</a> pole time of 1:04.871.
</p><p>The South African Grand Prix would only return in <a href="/wiki/1992_South_African_Grand_Prix" title="1992 South African Grand Prix">1992</a>, after apartheid ended, in a new configuration of the Kyalami circuit. Mansell would also win the 1992 race driving a Williams, albeit with a naturally-aspirated Renault engine.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
