<p>The <b>1985 Australian Grand Prix</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> <a href="/wiki/Motor_race" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor race">motor race</a> held on a <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Street_Circuit" title="Adelaide Street Circuit">street circuit</a> in the city of <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a> on 3 November 1985. The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Grand_Prix" title="Australian Grand Prix">Australian Grand Prix</a> was the sixteenth and final race of the <a href="/wiki/1985_FIA_Formula_One_World_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="1985 FIA Formula One World Championship">1985 FIA Formula One World Championship</a>. It was the 50th running of the Australian Grand Prix and the first to be held on the streets of Adelaide on a layout specifically designed for the debut of the World Championship in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. The race was held over 82 laps of the 3.780&#160;km (2.362&#160;mi) circuit for a total race distance of 310 kilometres. The race was won by <a href="/wiki/Keke_Rosberg" title="Keke Rosberg">Keke Rosberg</a> driving a <a href="/wiki/WilliamsF1" class="mw-redirect" title="WilliamsF1">Williams</a>-<a href="/wiki/Honda_F1" class="mw-redirect" title="Honda F1">Honda</a>; this was the final win by a <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finnish</a> driver until <a href="/wiki/Mika_H%C3%A4kkinen" title="Mika Häkkinen">Mika Häkkinen</a> won the <a href="/wiki/1997_European_Grand_Prix" title="1997 European Grand Prix">1997 European Grand Prix</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-race">Pre-race</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1985_Australian_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pre-race">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The new circuit was received extremely positively with glowing reviews from those within the paddock despite the circuit's temporary nature as it wound through streets, parkland and across horse racing venue <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Park_Racecourse" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria Park Racecourse">Victoria Park Racecourse</a> immediately adjacent to the Adelaide central business district, with the drivers enjoying a street circuit that was unlike <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_Monaco" title="Circuit de Monaco">Monaco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Street_Circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Detroit Street Circuit">Detroit</a> with their endless short straights, narrow roads and hairpin or right angle corners. The Adelaide circuit was wide and fast in places, and included a 900-metre long straight (named the "Brabham Straight" for Australia's three-time <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_World_Drivers%27_Champions" title="List of Formula One World Drivers&#39; Champions">World Champion</a> <a href="/wiki/Sir_Jack_Brabham" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Jack Brabham">Sir Jack Brabham</a>) where the faster cars reached over 200&#160;mph (322&#160;km/h). The reception for the track, and the professional way in which the event was organised and executed was sufficiently positive to see the promoters awarded the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_Promotional_Trophy_winners" title="List of Formula One Promotional Trophy winners">Formula One Promotional Trophy</a> for 1985.
</p><p>Dual World Champion <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Piquet" title="Nelson Piquet">Nelson Piquet</a> confirmed the drivers' positive view on the circuit when he said early in race week "After <a href="/wiki/Fair_Park#Formula_1" title="Fair Park">Dallas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caesars_Palace_Grand_Prix" title="Caesars Palace Grand Prix">Las Vegas</a>, we all expected another bad street circuit", while his <a href="/wiki/Brabham" title="Brabham">Brabham</a> team boss and head of the <a href="/wiki/Formula_One_Constructors_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Formula One Constructors Association">Formula One Constructors Association</a> (FOCA) <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Ecclestone" title="Bernie Ecclestone">Bernie Ecclestone</a> told the assembled media that he believed that the standard of the organisation and the circuit itself was bad news for Formula One, explaining that Adelaide had raised the standards of what would be expected in the future and that several tracks in Europe already on the calendar, or hoping to be, would have to lift their own games in order to match it. Over the course of the weekend, the only complaint from the drivers was of a lack of grip on the newly laid surface (along with the new road built inside the Victoria Park Racecourse which is where the pits were located, the entire circuit other than the Brabham Straight had been re-laid a few months prior to the race to prevent the problems often faced on American street circuits where the road surface broke up badly under the strain of the high powered cars). The new surface was causing graining in both qualifying and race tyres. Other than a bump in the road at the end of the Brabham Straight, the circuit itself was generally given the thumbs up by those that really mattered, the teams and their often highly paid drivers.
</p><p>The only Australian driver in the field, <a href="/wiki/1980_Formula_One_season" title="1980 Formula One season">1980</a> World Drivers' Champion <a href="/wiki/Alan_Jones_(Formula_1)" class="mw-redirect" title="Alan Jones (Formula 1)">Alan Jones</a> who was driving the <a href="/wiki/Haas_Lola" title="Haas Lola">Haas Lola</a> team's <a href="/wiki/Lola_THL1" title="Lola THL1">Lola THL1</a>-<a href="/wiki/Brian_Hart_Ltd." class="mw-redirect" title="Brian Hart Ltd.">Hart</a>, was given the honour of driving the first Formula One car out onto the new circuit when first practice opened at 10am on the Friday morning. Bernie Ecclestone had arranged for Jones to do a lap of the track before any other cars were released from the pits. Jones also had the honour of having a section of track named after him, with the <a href="/wiki/Rundle_Street,_Adelaide" title="Rundle Street, Adelaide">Rundle Road</a> section of the track, a 350-metre straight between turns 9 and 10, renamed as the "Jones Straight" when the circuit was in use. Jones Straight led directly onto the fast Brabham Straight.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualifying">Qualifying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1985_Australian_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Qualifying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Official qualifying became a battle royal between the <a href="/wiki/Williams_F1" class="mw-redirect" title="Williams F1">Williams</a>-<a href="/wiki/Honda_Racing_F1" class="mw-redirect" title="Honda Racing F1">Hondas</a> of <a href="/wiki/1982_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="1982 Formula One World Championship">1982</a> World Champion <a href="/wiki/Keke_Rosberg" title="Keke Rosberg">Keke Rosberg</a> and his teammate <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Team_Lotus" title="Team Lotus">Lotus</a>-<a href="/wiki/Renault_F1" class="mw-redirect" title="Renault F1">Renault</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a>. Rosberg took the honors on Friday with a lap of 1:22.402, only 0.001 faster than Senna, with Mansell a further 0.161 back in third. During final qualifying, Mansell looked to have pole position wrapped up with a lap of 1:20.537 in his <a href="/wiki/Williams_FW10" title="Williams FW10">Williams FW10</a>, with Senna second with Rosberg another three-tenths further back in third. Senna was out on his third run with only minutes left before the end of qualifying in his black and gold <a href="/wiki/Lotus_97T" title="Lotus 97T">Lotus 97T</a>, powered by a special Renault qualifying engine rumored to be putting out some 1,150&#160;bhp (858&#160;kW; 1,166&#160;PS), and stunned everyone with a time of 1:19.843, seven-tenths faster than Mansell, to grab his 7th pole position of the season. As such, Senna was the only driver to record a time under 1:20.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>World Champion elect <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a> was 4th on the grid in his <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/2" title="McLaren MP4/2">McLaren</a> <a href="/wiki/Techniques_d%27Avant_Garde" title="Techniques d&#39;Avant Garde">TAG</a>-<a href="/wiki/Porsche_in_Formula_One" class="mw-redirect" title="Porsche in Formula One">Porsche</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_156/85" title="Ferrari 156/85">Ferrari</a> of 1985 World Championship runner up <a href="/wiki/Michele_Alboreto" title="Michele Alboreto">Michele Alboreto</a> fifth, and <a href="/wiki/Marc_Surer" title="Marc Surer">Marc Surer</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Brabham_BT54" title="Brabham BT54">Brabham</a>-<a href="/wiki/BMW_M12" title="BMW M12">BMW</a> rounding out the top six qualifiers. Outgoing World Champion <a href="/wiki/Niki_Lauda" title="Niki Lauda">Niki Lauda</a> was 16th on the grid in his McLaren. Alan Jones ended up 19th on the grid after engine and turbo problems throughout practice and qualifying.
</p><p>The slowest qualifier for the race, <a href="/wiki/Huub_Rothengatter" title="Huub Rothengatter">Huub Rothengatter</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Osella" title="Osella">Osella</a>-<a href="/wiki/Alfa_Romeo_in_Formula_One" title="Alfa Romeo in Formula One">Alfa Romeo</a>, was 10.473 seconds slower than Senna with a time of 1:30.319. With only 25 cars appearing for the race (the <a href="/wiki/RAM_Racing" title="RAM Racing">RAM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zakspeed" title="Zakspeed">Zakspeed</a> teams did not contest the last two races of the season - indeed RAM would never race Formula One again), all cars that attended qualified for the start.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Race">Race</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1985_Australian_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Race">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The race of high attrition, which was run in 35 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C heat</a>, was won by Rosberg driving the Williams-Honda; it would turn out to be the last victory (and last fastest lap) of his Formula One career. Rosberg was the only front running driver to finish strongly and one of only eight cars to be classified. It was the fifth and final Grand Prix win of Rosberg's career and confirmed his status as a street race specialist with three of his previous four wins being on the streets of <a href="/wiki/Monaco_Grand_Prix" title="Monaco Grand Prix">Monaco</a> (<a href="/wiki/1983_Monaco_Grand_Prix" title="1983 Monaco Grand Prix">1983</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Grand_Prix" title="Dallas Grand Prix">Dallas</a> (1984), and <a href="/wiki/1985_Detroit_Grand_Prix" title="1985 Detroit Grand Prix">Detroit</a> earlier in the 1985 season. Rosberg had some technical problems which could have lost him the race, his race engineer, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Dernie" title="Frank Dernie">Frank Dernie</a> suggested a strategy to minimise loss of time. Rosberg gave the winner's trophy to Dernie.
</p><p>On the penultimate lap, <a href="/wiki/Equipe_Ligier" title="Equipe Ligier">Ligier</a>-Renault driver <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Streiff" title="Philippe Streiff">Philippe Streiff</a> tried to overtake his senior team-mate <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Laffite" title="Jacques Laffite">Jacques Laffite</a> for second place and, as result of this manoeuvre, Streiff's front wheel axle was severely damaged. With fourth placed <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Capelli" title="Ivan Capelli">Ivan Capelli</a> a lap down in his <a href="/wiki/Tyrrell_Racing" title="Tyrrell Racing">Tyrrell</a>-Renault, Streiff managed to limp his <a href="/wiki/Ligier_JS25" title="Ligier JS25">Ligier JS25</a> home and retain third place despite only have three wheels firmly attached to the car, with the front left wheel bouncing up and down over the course of the last lap, but somehow remaining attached to the car. It would remain a career best finish for Streiff. Ligier team boss <a href="/wiki/Guy_Ligier" title="Guy Ligier">Guy Ligier</a>, not impressed that Streiff had almost taken out both team cars at the end of the race when they were both assured of a podium finish, would not offer the Frenchman a drive for <a href="/wiki/1986_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="1986 Formula One World Championship">1986</a>.
</p><p>A delayed <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Johansson" title="Stefan Johansson">Stefan Johansson</a> finished fifth in his Ferrari ahead of <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Berger" title="Gerhard Berger">Gerhard Berger</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Arrows_A8" title="Arrows A8">Arrows</a>-BMW. Berger at that stage of his career was also a part-time factory <a href="/wiki/Touring_car_racing" title="Touring car racing">touring car</a> driver for <a href="/wiki/BMW_in_motorsport" title="BMW in motorsport">BMW</a> in the <a href="/wiki/European_Touring_Car_Championship" title="European Touring Car Championship">European Touring Car Championship</a> (he had won the <a href="/wiki/Spa_24_Hours" title="Spa 24 Hours">Spa 24 Hours</a> for BMW's <a href="/wiki/Schnitzer_Motorsport" title="Schnitzer Motorsport">Schnitzer Motorsport</a> in July 1985). He performed double duty during the AGP weekend, also driving a <a href="/wiki/BMW_E24" class="mw-redirect" title="BMW E24">BMW 635 CSi</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Group_A" title="Group A">Group A</a> support race. This was actually a breach of Formula One's driver rules which stated a driver must not drive another type of race car or in another motor race within the 24 hours before the start of a Grand Prix. As the Group A race was scheduled to start on the Saturday afternoon one hour after F1 qualifying had finished, Berger had to obtain permission from <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_du_Sport_Automobile" title="Fédération Internationale du Sport Automobile">FISA</a>, FOCA, and his <a href="/wiki/Arrows_Grand_Prix_International" title="Arrows Grand Prix International">Arrows</a> team boss <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Oliver" title="Jackie Oliver">Jackie Oliver</a> to race the BMW. As it turned out, his touring car race lasted 3 laps before he was punted into the gravel trap at the end of the pit straight by the <a href="/wiki/Mobil_Holden_Dealer_Team" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobil Holden Dealer Team">Mobil Holden Dealer Team</a> <a href="/wiki/Holden_Commodore_SS_Group_A" class="mw-redirect" title="Holden Commodore SS Group A">Commodore</a> <a href="/wiki/Holden_V8_engine" title="Holden V8 engine">V8</a> of Australian veteran <a href="/wiki/John_Harvey_(racing_driver)" title="John Harvey (racing driver)">John Harvey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The only other race finishers were Huub Rothengatter (Osella) and <a href="/wiki/Pierluigi_Martini" title="Pierluigi Martini">Pierluigi Martini</a> (<a href="/wiki/Minardi" title="Minardi">Minardi</a>), both of whom were four laps behind Rosberg. <a href="/wiki/Martin_Brundle" title="Martin Brundle">Martin Brundle</a>'s Tyrrell was running at the end, but Brundle was 33 laps down on Rosberg and was not classified as a finisher.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Renault_F1" class="mw-redirect" title="Renault F1">Renault</a> had a largely forgettable weekend in their last Grand Prix as a manufacturer (until <a href="/wiki/2002_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="2002 Formula One World Championship">2002</a>). <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Tambay" title="Patrick Tambay">Patrick Tambay</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Renault_RE60" title="Renault RE60">Renault RE60B</a> was fitted with an on-board camera during practice and qualifying, qualified 8th while <a href="/wiki/Derek_Warwick" title="Derek Warwick">Derek Warwick</a> qualified in 12th place. After Friday's qualifying, Tambay visited Adelaide's major <a href="/wiki/Dirt_track_racing" title="Dirt track racing">dirt track racing</a> venue <a href="/wiki/Speedway_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Speedway City">Speedway Park</a> which was hosting the inaugural Australian <a href="/wiki/Sprint_car_racing" title="Sprint car racing">Sprintcar</a> Masters meeting and while there turned a few laps of the ¼ mile <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> oval track in a 700&#160;hp (522&#160;kW; 710&#160;PS) <a href="/wiki/V8_engine" title="V8 engine">V8</a> Sprintcar. Warwick did the same thing on the Saturday night of the two night meeting. However, unlike Berger in the Group A race, Warwick did not have permission to drive the winged sprint car within the 24 hours before the race time period and was hit with a hefty fine by the governing body FISA (despite Warwick only driving a few laps and not actually racing). In their last race both Renaults retired with transmission failure, Tambay on lap 21 and Warwick on lap 58.
</p><p>All 25 cars present qualified for the race. With RAM Racing (who pulled out of F1 altogether after the <a href="/wiki/1985_European_Grand_Prix" title="1985 European Grand Prix">European Grand Prix</a>) and Zakspeed (who only ran the European races in 1985) not making the journey to Australia, there would not be a cut-off based on grid numbers. Early in the race <a href="/wiki/Elio_de_Angelis" title="Elio de Angelis">Elio de Angelis</a>, driving in his last race for Lotus, was disqualified for regaining his original grid position after being delayed on the parade lap. de Angelis later confessed that in the heat of the moment he simply forgot the rules.
</p><p>The race was the 100th World Championship start for 1980 World Champion Alan Jones. Jones (along with Alain Prost) was one of two drivers to have won the Australian Grand Prix previously (Jones in <a href="/wiki/1980_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1980 Australian Grand Prix">1980</a> and Prost in <a href="/wiki/1982_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1982 Australian Grand Prix">1982</a>). Jones' Lola retired on lap 21 with failed electrics following a typically fiery drive through to 6th place after stalling at the start and being dead last at the end of the first lap, while Prost retired with engine failure on lap 27. It was also the last race for outgoing world champion Niki Lauda. His McLaren ended in the fence with damage to the front left after a brake failure at the end of the long Brabham Straight.
</p><p>Fittingly Lauda's career ended while he was in the lead of a Grand Prix. Lauda and race winner Rosberg were the only drivers in the field who had actually competed in the non-championship <a href="/wiki/1984_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1984 Australian Grand Prix">1984 Australian Grand Prix</a> which was held for <a href="/wiki/Formula_Pacific" title="Formula Pacific">Formula Pacific</a> cars. Lauda had also failed to finish the 1984 race while Rosberg had finished second behind Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Moreno" title="Roberto Moreno">Roberto Moreno</a>.
</p><p>Both <a href="/wiki/Alfa_Romeo_in_Formula_One" title="Alfa Romeo in Formula One">Alfa Romeo</a> and Renault had their last Grand Prix as a constructor in the turbo era. As of <a href="/wiki/2020_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="2020 Formula One World Championship">2020</a>, Alfa have never returned to Grand Prix racing as a factory team; the Alfa team that competed in F1 from 1982-1985 was actually a pseudo-factory team actually run by <a href="/w/index.php?title=EuroRacing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="EuroRacing (page does not exist)">EuroRacing</a> with support from the Alfa factory, and the Alfa team that will take part in the 2019 F1 season is a rebranded <a href="/wiki/Sauber" class="mw-redirect" title="Sauber">Sauber</a> with Alfa Romeo providing technical assistance with the hope that Alfa Romeo will be a works team in the near future.<sup id="cite_ref-MW1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MW1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Renault would return in <a href="/wiki/2002_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="2002 Formula One World Championship">2002</a> following their purchase the <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Formula" title="Benetton Formula">Benetton</a> team. It was also the last Grand Prix for the <a href="/wiki/Toleman" title="Toleman">Toleman</a> team under the Toleman name. The team would continue in <a href="/wiki/1986_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="1986 Formula One World Championship">1986</a> but would be renamed Benetton after being purchased by their main sponsors, the Italian clothes manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Group" title="Benetton Group">Benetton Group</a>. All six cars (the <a href="/wiki/Alfa_Romeo_184T" title="Alfa Romeo 184T">Alfa Romeo 184TB</a>'s of <a href="/wiki/Riccardo_Patrese" title="Riccardo Patrese">Riccardo Patrese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Cheever" title="Eddie Cheever">Eddie Cheever</a>, the Renault RE60B's of Patrick Tambay and Derek Warwick, and the Hart powered <a href="/wiki/Toleman_TG185" title="Toleman TG185">Toleman TG185</a>'s of <a href="/wiki/Teo_Fabi" title="Teo Fabi">Teo Fabi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piercarlo_Ghinzani" title="Piercarlo Ghinzani">Piercarlo Ghinzani</a>) failed to finish the last F1 race for their teams.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_notes">Cultural notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1985_Australian_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Adelaide's northern industrial suburb Wingfield, a connected group of streets bears the names of the 1985 pole-sitter and the 6 points scorers: Senna, Rosberg, Laffite, Streiff, Capelli, Johansson and Berger Roads.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
