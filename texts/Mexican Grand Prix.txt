<p>The <b>Mexican Grand Prix</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Gran Premio de México</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile" title="Fédération Internationale de l&#39;Automobile">FIA</a>-sanctioned <a href="/wiki/Auto_racing" title="Auto racing">auto race</a> held at the <a href="/wiki/Aut%C3%B3dromo_Hermanos_Rodr%C3%ADguez" title="Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez">Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>. It first appeared as a non-championship event in 1962 before being held as a championship event in 1963–1970 and 1986–1992. The Grand Prix returned in <a href="/wiki/2015_Mexican_Grand_Prix" title="2015 Mexican Grand Prix">2015</a> at the Mexico City circuit.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Magdalena_Mixhuca_.281962.E2.80.931970.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Magdalena_Mixhuca_(1962–1970)">Magdalena Mixhuca (1962–1970)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Magdalena Mixhuca (1962–1970)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Mexican Grand Prix was first held on 4 November 1962 at the <a href="/wiki/Aut%C3%B3dromo_Hermanos_Rodr%C3%ADguez" title="Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez">Magdalena Mixhuca</a> circuit. The circuit was the first international racetrack in Mexico and like <a href="/wiki/Autodromo_Nazionale_Monza" class="mw-redirect" title="Autodromo Nazionale Monza">Monza</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, Italy it was built within a park in the center part of a major city, this city of course being the Mexican capital of <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>. The race provided unique challenges for racing, standing at 2,240m (7,340 ft) above sea level, as well as the long, 180-degree, lightly banked and fast Peraltada corner that finishes the lap, in addition to being a bumpy racetrack from actively shifting soils beneath the circuit. The Mexican Grand Prix of this period was always the season finale Grand Prix, held in late October.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/1962_Mexican_Grand_Prix" title="1962 Mexican Grand Prix">first race</a>, a non-championship affair which attracted a strong international entry, was won by <a href="/wiki/Team_Lotus" title="Team Lotus">Team Lotus</a> with <a href="/wiki/Jim_Clark" title="Jim Clark">Jim Clark</a> taking over the car of teammate <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Taylor_(racing_driver)" title="Trevor Taylor (racing driver)">Trevor Taylor</a> to claim the win; Clark was black-flagged after receiving a push-start at a confused race start. The meeting was marred by the death of young Mexican star <a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Rodr%C3%ADguez_(racing_driver)" title="Ricardo Rodríguez (racing driver)">Ricardo Rodríguez</a>, killed in practice in a Rob Walker run <a href="/wiki/Lotus_24" title="Lotus 24">Lotus 24</a> on the Peraltada.
</p><p>The Formula One World Championship arrived the following year with Clark winning again, equalling <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel_Fangio" title="Juan Manuel Fangio">Juan Manuel Fangio</a>'s record of most victories in a single season. 1964 saw the battle for both the Drivers' and Constructors' championships. British drivers Clark, <a href="/wiki/John_Surtees" title="John Surtees">John Surtees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graham_Hill" title="Graham Hill">Graham Hill</a> all arrived with a chance, with Hill leading the table; and Ferrari, BRM and Lotus were in contention for the Constructors' Championship. Ferrari signalled <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Bandini" title="Lorenzo Bandini">Lorenzo Bandini</a> to let teammate Surtees through, which he did, and Surtees finished second behind Gurney to win the championship by one point; Ferrari won the Constructors' Championship.
</p><p>American <a href="/wiki/Richie_Ginther" title="Richie Ginther">Richie Ginther</a> took victory for Honda in 1965, the Japanese company's first win in Formula One. Clark won his third Mexican Grand Prix in 1967, making him the most frequent winner of the race as of 2019.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 1968, three men again came into the race with a chance of winning the Drivers' Championship. Hill, his countryman <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Stewart" title="Jackie Stewart">Jackie Stewart</a>, and New Zealander and defending world champion <a href="/wiki/Denny_Hulme" title="Denny Hulme">Denny Hulme</a>. The race was a straight fight between Hill and Stewart; the Scotsman led for several laps until Hill passed him. Hulme was running third, but he had a rear suspension failure and crashed on Lap 11. Swiss <a href="/wiki/Jo_Siffert" title="Jo Siffert">Jo Siffert</a> decided to get in on the mix and took the lead, but he had to pit with a broken throttle cable. Stewart then fell back with when his engine started to misfire, his car's handling was going off and the car also had a fuel-feed problem. But Hill had no problems; and took victory and his second driver's championship.
</p><p>Crowd control in 1968 and 1970 contributed to cancellation of the event.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1970, a record crowd of approximately 200,000 arrived to see <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Rodr%C3%ADguez_(racing_driver)" title="Pedro Rodríguez (racing driver)">Pedro Rodríguez</a>, forcing officials to delay the race start by an hour as they struggled to control the crowd.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> At one point a dog ran across the track and was hit by Stewart. During the race, spectators threw bottles on the track.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Amid the chaos, <a href="/wiki/Clay_Regazzoni" title="Clay Regazzoni">Clay Regazzoni</a> trailed <a href="/wiki/Jacky_Ickx" title="Jacky Ickx">Jacky Ickx</a> for a 1-2 Ferrari finish.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1971 event was scheduled, with large fund deposited in a Swiss bank to help guarantee better crowd control, but after the death of Pedro Rodriguez, the plan was abandoned.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Aut.C3.B3dromo_Hermanos_Rodr.C3.ADguez_.281986.E2.80.931992.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Autódromo_Hermanos_Rodríguez_(1986–1992)">Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez (1986–1992)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez (1986–1992)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gran_Premio_de_M%C3%A9xico_Logo.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Gran_Premio_de_M%C3%A9xico_Logo.png/210px-Gran_Premio_de_M%C3%A9xico_Logo.png" decoding="async" width="210" height="107" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Gran_Premio_de_M%C3%A9xico_Logo.png/315px-Gran_Premio_de_M%C3%A9xico_Logo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Gran_Premio_de_M%C3%A9xico_Logo.png/420px-Gran_Premio_de_M%C3%A9xico_Logo.png 2x" data-file-width="555" data-file-height="282" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gran_Premio_de_M%C3%A9xico_Logo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Classic Mexican GP event logo.</div></div></div><p> There had been a number of attempts to bring the Mexican Grand Prix back on the F1 calendar, one attempt being when it was originally scheduled in April 1980, two weeks after the US Grand Prix West, but was cancelled. American IndyCars arrived for a brief two-year visit in 1980 and 1981, racing as the <a href="/wiki/Gran_Premio_Tecate" title="Gran Premio Tecate">Gran Premio Tecate</a> on the Magdalena Mixhuca track now named for Mexico's two lost racing heroes, Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez. The event was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Rick_Mears" title="Rick Mears">Rick Mears</a>. A number of years later, work began on rebuilding the Hermanos Rodríguez circuit with much improved organisation. The circuit's layout was slightly shorter, the Peraltada's banking had been eased and the circuit was generally much safer than it had been. The Grand Prix returned in 1986 where the race played host to Austrian <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Berger" title="Gerhard Berger">Gerhard Berger</a>'s first race win in his <a href="/wiki/Benetton_B186" title="Benetton B186">Benetton B186</a>, in a race where an ill Berger outlasted his opposition as tyre problems struck most of the field. The circuit was still very rough and bumpy, however. 1987 saw the race being run in two parts. It was stopped around mid-distance when Briton <a href="/wiki/Derek_Warwick" title="Derek Warwick">Derek Warwick</a> crashed heavily coming out of the Peraltada. Brazilian driver <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Piquet" title="Nelson Piquet">Nelson Piquet</a> finished first on the road, but because his Williams teammate <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a> was 30 seconds ahead when the first race ended, Mansell kept Piquet in sight and won the race on corrected time.
</p><p>The 1988 race was moved from mid-October to a warmer and more-rain prone late May season slot, so that it could be paired with the other North American Grands Prix in Montreal and Detroit. This race saw Frenchman Alain Prost dominate in his McLaren, and Prost's Brazilian teammate <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a> won the next year; this was at a time when the two men's relationship was at a low point. 1990 saw the race moved to late June. Prost, now at Ferrari, qualified 13th on the grid but drove through the field, and took second from his teammate Mansell late in the race. Senna, who was leading, had a slow puncture that turned into shredded rubber and he went into the pits to have it changed, but the suspension was too badly damaged for the Brazilian to continue. This put Prost and Mansell 1–2 but Senna's teammate Gerhard Berger was challenging Mansell for second; Berger passed the Englishman going into the <a href="/wiki/Moises_Solana" class="mw-redirect" title="Moises Solana">Moises Solana</a> esses, but Mansell repassed him around the outside of the Peraltada on the same lap. Prost won the race; Mansell and Berger finished second and third. 1991 saw Senna crash heavily at the Peraltada during practice; he was declared fit to race by FIA doctor <a href="/wiki/Sid_Watkins" title="Sid Watkins">Sid Watkins</a>; he finished third behind Williams drivers <a href="/wiki/Riccardo_Patrese" title="Riccardo Patrese">Riccardo Patrese</a> and Mansell; but Senna had been critical of the severely bumpy circuit all of that weekend. On 9 October 1991 European media sources reported that promoters barely made enough funds to pay off F1 for the 1991 race. FISA demanded improvements to the track for the 1992 event, which were made.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For the 1992 season, the race had been moved further forward to March, and on 20 February of that year, Mexico City's air pollution had reached a record level. City officials had imposed emergency measures banning half of government cars and equipment from the streets; this was not helped by the fact that Mexico City is situated in a valley surrounded by mountains, which restricts polluted air to travel elsewhere to dissipate. This put extra stress on the Mexican Grand Prix committee to ensure the track was ready for 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Some safety measures had been implemented to the track, including further easing of the banking at Peraltada, making the corner a little slower; this was done to avoid bypassing the entry to this corner via making an alternate route to the middle of the Peraltada through the park's baseball stadium. Although the circuit was popular with drivers, they began complaining during interviews with the press about the ubiquitous and severe bumps on the circuit, which had decayed even further and were even worse than before- even the Peraltada was very bumpy and Mansell nearly crashed there during practice. Even with these concerns the race that year went ahead, and it saw Williams teammates Mansell and Patrese dominate the race. Senna had another bad accident during practice, this time at the fast Esses- Senna went over a nasty series of bumps that unsettled the car to the point where Senna lost control, went off the track and hit a concrete wall; he had minor injuries but qualified 6th and took part in the race. But the state of the track, the decline of Mexico City itself, not only with air pollution problems but also a rapid and unstable city population increase saw Formula One leave again.
</p>
<h3><span id="Absence_.281993.E2.80.932014.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Absence_(1993–2014)">Absence (1993–2014)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Absence (1993–2014)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>2002 saw the revival of the Grand Prix of Mexico for Champ Cars on a much modified version of the Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez circuit which included cutting the <i>peraltada</i> in half. This was a six-year stay which saw <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9bastien_Bourdais" title="Sébastien Bourdais">Sébastien Bourdais</a> win half of the six races that followed.
</p><p>Rumors first surfaced in 2003 that the Mexican Grand Prix might return to the <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> calendar at a new <a href="/wiki/Dollar" title="Dollar">$</a>70 <a href="/wiki/Million" class="mw-redirect" title="Million">million</a> circuit, dubbed "<a href="/wiki/Mobula" title="Mobula">Mantarraya</a>", to be built near <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Cancún">Cancún</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, the governor of <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a> state said that Mexico would have a Grand Prix on the calendar for <a href="/wiki/2006_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="2006 Formula One World Championship">2006</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The plan was halted later that year as a debate arose about whether the land the circuit was to be built on was properly owned by the right people to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/2006_United_States_Grand_Prix" title="2006 United States Grand Prix">2006 United States Grand Prix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Ecclestone" title="Bernie Ecclestone">Bernie Ecclestone</a> announced that the Grand Prix of Mexico would return for the <a href="/wiki/2009_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="2009 Formula One World Championship">2009</a> season, but nothing further came from the announcement.
</p>
<h3><span id="Return_to_the_Aut.C3.B3dromo_Hermanos_Rodr.C3.ADguez_.282015.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_the_Autódromo_Hermanos_Rodríguez_(2015–present)">Return to the Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez (2015–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Return to the Autódromo Hermanos Rodríguez (2015–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FIA_F1_Austria_2018_Nr._44_Hamilton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/FIA_F1_Austria_2018_Nr._44_Hamilton.jpg/220px-FIA_F1_Austria_2018_Nr._44_Hamilton.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/FIA_F1_Austria_2018_Nr._44_Hamilton.jpg/330px-FIA_F1_Austria_2018_Nr._44_Hamilton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/FIA_F1_Austria_2018_Nr._44_Hamilton.jpg/440px-FIA_F1_Austria_2018_Nr._44_Hamilton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4086" data-file-height="2298" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FIA_F1_Austria_2018_Nr._44_Hamilton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Five-time World Champion <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Hamilton" title="Lewis Hamilton">Lewis Hamilton</a> clinched his fourth and fifth titles in <a href="/wiki/2017_Mexican_Grand_Prix" title="2017 Mexican Grand Prix">2017</a> and <a href="/wiki/2018_Mexican_Grand_Prix" title="2018 Mexican Grand Prix">2018</a> respectively at the Mexican Grand Prix.</div></div></div>
<p>In August 2011, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Slim_Domit" title="Carlos Slim Domit">Carlos Slim Domit</a> revealed plans for a revived race.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In August 2013, it was suggested by "high level sources" that the Mexican Grand Prix could be on the provisional 2014 World Championship calendar.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A preliminary draft calendar for the 2014 season, circulated in early September 2013, assigned 9 November 2014 for the Mexican Grand Prix, but did not specify a circuit and noted that the event was "subject to confirmation".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> But on 5 December 2013, the FIA released the official 2014 Formula One season calendar, and the Mexican Grand Prix was not on the calendar; then the FIA announced that the Mexican Grand Prix was postponed to 2015 due to lack of sufficient preparation time to upgrade the somewhat run-down Hermanos Rodríguez circuit to Formula 1 working standards.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In July 2014, Ecclestone confirmed that he had signed a 5-year deal for the Hermanos Rodríguez track to host the Mexican Grand Prix, starting in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On 3 December 2014, the FIA published a confirmed calendar for 2015<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> showing the 2015 Grand Prix of Mexico on 1 November 2015. German Nico Rosberg won the 2015 event in his Mercedes.
</p><p>On 14 May 2019, Mexico City mayor <a href="/wiki/Claudia_Sheinbaum" title="Claudia Sheinbaum">Claudia Sheinbaum</a> announced that 2019 would be the last year for the Grand Prix of Mexico because the MXN $400&#160;million (US$20.865&#160;million) fee was to be invested in the <a href="/wiki/Mayan_Train" title="Mayan Train">Tren Maya</a>. It is estimated that the race generates MXN $8,400&#160;million to the local economy.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, on 8 August it was announced that the Mexican Grand Prix would remain on the calendar until 2022, though it would be renamed to "Mexico City Grand Prix".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
