<p>The <b>1989 Monaco Grand Prix</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> motor race held at the <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_Monaco" title="Circuit de Monaco">Circuit de Monaco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo" title="Monte Carlo">Monte Carlo</a> on 7 May 1989. It was the third race of the <a href="/wiki/1989_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="1989 Formula One World Championship">1989 Formula One World Championship</a>. The 77-lap race was won from <a href="/wiki/Pole_position" title="Pole position">pole position</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a>, driving a <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/5" title="McLaren MP4/5">McLaren</a>-<a href="/wiki/Honda_in_Formula_One" title="Honda in Formula One">Honda</a>, with teammate <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a> second and <a href="/wiki/Stefano_Modena" title="Stefano Modena">Stefano Modena</a> third in a <a href="/wiki/Brabham_BT58" title="Brabham BT58">Brabham</a>-<a href="/wiki/Judd_(engine)" title="Judd (engine)">Judd</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualifying">Qualifying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1989_Monaco_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Qualifying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-qualifying_report">Pre-qualifying report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1989_Monaco_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-qualifying report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The field was one fewer in Monaco as <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Ferrari</a> had elected not to run a second car to replace <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Berger" title="Gerhard Berger">Gerhard Berger</a>, who had been injured in an accident during the last race at <a href="/wiki/1989_San_Marino_Grand_Prix" title="1989 San Marino Grand Prix">Imola</a>. However, unlike the similar situation at the first race in <a href="/wiki/1989_Brazilian_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Brazilian Grand Prix">Brazil</a>, no extra pre-qualifier would be allowed through to the main qualifying sessions, which here at Monaco would run with 29 cars.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Brabham" title="Brabham">Brabham</a> again topped the time sheets during the Thursday morning pre-qualifying session, with <a href="/wiki/Stefano_Modena" title="Stefano Modena">Stefano Modena</a> fastest, but the <a href="/wiki/Dallara" title="Dallara">Dallara</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alex_Caffi" title="Alex Caffi">Alex Caffi</a> was only 0.141 seconds behind. Third was <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Henri_Raphanel" title="Pierre-Henri Raphanel">Pierre-Henri Raphanel</a>, who put in a fine performance in his <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Coloni" title="Scuderia Coloni">Coloni</a>, pre-qualifying for the first, and ultimately, only time. The fourth pre-qualifier was the other Brabham, driven by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Brundle" title="Martin Brundle">Martin Brundle</a>, who edged out the <a href="/wiki/Osella" title="Osella">Osella</a> of <a href="/wiki/Piercarlo_Ghinzani" title="Piercarlo Ghinzani">Piercarlo Ghinzani</a> by just two-hundredths of a second.
</p><p>Joining Ghinzani on the sidelines were <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Johansson" title="Stefan Johansson">Stefan Johansson</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Onyx_Grand_Prix" title="Onyx Grand Prix">Onyx</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Larini" title="Nicola Larini">Nicola Larini</a> in the other Osella, followed by <a href="/wiki/Bernd_Schneider_(racing_driver)" title="Bernd Schneider (racing driver)">Bernd Schneider</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Zakspeed" title="Zakspeed">Zakspeed</a>. Ninth was the other Onyx of <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Gachot" title="Bertrand Gachot">Bertrand Gachot</a>, ahead of the sole <a href="/wiki/EuroBrun" title="EuroBrun">EuroBrun</a> driven by <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Foitek" title="Gregor Foitek">Gregor Foitek</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Rial_Racing" title="Rial Racing">Rial</a> of <a href="/wiki/Volker_Weidler" title="Volker Weidler">Volker Weidler</a> was eleventh, followed by <a href="/wiki/Aguri_Suzuki" title="Aguri Suzuki">Aguri Suzuki</a> in the other Zakspeed. Slowest on this occasion was <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Winkelhock" title="Joachim Winkelhock">Joachim Winkelhock</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Automobiles_Gonfaronnaises_Sportives" title="Automobiles Gonfaronnaises Sportives">AGS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualifying_report">Qualifying report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1989_Monaco_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Qualifying report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tyrrell_Racing" title="Tyrrell Racing">Tyrrell</a> had new cars that looked promising. Ayrton Senna was on pole by a full second over teammate Alain Prost with <a href="/wiki/Thierry_Boutsen" title="Thierry Boutsen">Thierry Boutsen</a> sharing row two with the surprisingly competitive <a href="/wiki/Brabham" title="Brabham">Brabham</a> of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Brundle" title="Martin Brundle">Martin Brundle</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a> was fifth followed by <a href="/wiki/Derek_Warwick" title="Derek Warwick">Derek Warwick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riccardo_Patrese" title="Riccardo Patrese">Riccardo Patrese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stefano_Modena" title="Stefano Modena">Stefano Modena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Caffi" title="Alex Caffi">Alex Caffi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andrea_de_Cesaris" title="Andrea de Cesaris">Andrea de Cesaris</a>. 
</p><p>It was at this race that many in the paddock started noticing that the <a href="/wiki/Pirelli" title="Pirelli">Pirelli</a> qualifying tyres were superior to <a href="/wiki/Goodyear_Tire_and_Rubber_Company" title="Goodyear Tire and Rubber Company">Goodyear</a>'s (the Brabhams and Caffi's <a href="/wiki/Dallara" title="Dallara">Dallara</a> ran on Pirelli rubber).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Race">Race</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1989_Monaco_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Race">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Race_report">Race report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1989_Monaco_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Race report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the start Senna got away brilliantly and Prost could do nothing but slot into second on the tight circuit where pole position counts more than at any other circuit. The McLarens proceeded to pull away from the field as usual while behind them Williams were in all sorts of trouble, as both Boutsen and Patrese had to stop for new rear wings. Nigel Mansell went out on lap 20 with more gearbox issues for Ferrari and one of the talking points of the race came on lap 33 when de Cesaris attempted to pass <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Piquet" title="Nelson Piquet">Nelson Piquet</a> at Loews Hairpin. The predictable accident occurred and some choice words were exchanged between the two drivers (while still in their respective cars) and a huge traffic jam was caused. Brundle was looking good until he had to stop for a new battery and dropped back to seventh.
</p><p>Senna continued to dominate the race while Prost, having been slowed by the Piquet-de Cesaris incident (he lost over 20 seconds to Senna in one lap having to wait for clear road to get moving again), could not recover and finished second behind his team mate. He was also held up for many laps trying to lap the <a href="/wiki/Ligier_JS33" title="Ligier JS33">Ligier</a> of former <a href="/wiki/Renault_F1" class="mw-redirect" title="Renault F1">Renault</a> team mate <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Arnoux" title="René Arnoux">René Arnoux</a> who ignored both his mirrors and the blue flags prompting <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> commentator <a href="/wiki/James_Hunt" title="James Hunt">James Hunt</a> to describe Arnoux's explanation of why he was so slow as "Bullshit" on live television. It was Senna's second win at Monaco and he did it the hard way, his McLaren losing first and second gear later in the race and disguising it to his best so Prost wouldn't react and push for the lead. Modena benefited from Brundle's stop and finished third, scoring his first points in Formula One and Brabham's last ever podium finish. <a href="/wiki/Alex_Caffi" title="Alex Caffi">Alex Caffi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michele_Alboreto" title="Michele Alboreto">Michele Alboreto</a>, and Brundle, who was promoted to sixth on the final lap as a result of the retirement of <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Capelli" title="Ivan Capelli">Ivan Capelli</a>, completed the point scoring positions. Caffi achieved both his and Dallara's first points.
</p>
