<p>The <b>1997 German Grand Prix</b> (formally the <b>LIX Grosser Mobil 1 Preis von Deutschland</b>) was a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> motor race held at <a href="/wiki/Hockenheimring" title="Hockenheimring">Hockenheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> on 27 July 1997. It was the tenth race of the <a href="/wiki/1997_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="1997 Formula One World Championship">1997 Formula One World Championship</a>.
</p><p>The 45-lap race was won by Austrian <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Berger" title="Gerhard Berger">Gerhard Berger</a>, driving a <a href="/wiki/Benetton_B197" title="Benetton B197">Benetton</a>-<a href="/wiki/Renault_in_Formula_One" title="Renault in Formula One">Renault</a>. Having missed the previous three races due to a sinus problem, Berger took <a href="/wiki/Pole_position" title="Pole position">pole position</a> and led the race from start to finish, except for the pit stops. It was Berger's tenth and final Grand Prix victory, and the 27th and last for the Benetton team. Local driver <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schumacher" title="Michael Schumacher">Michael Schumacher</a> finished second in a <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_F310" title="Ferrari F310">Ferrari</a>, with Finn <a href="/wiki/Mika_H%C3%A4kkinen" title="Mika Häkkinen">Mika Häkkinen</a> third in a <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/12" title="McLaren MP4/12">McLaren</a>-<a href="/wiki/Mercedes_AMG_High_Performance_Powertrains" title="Mercedes AMG High Performance Powertrains">Mercedes</a>.
</p><p>With <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Villeneuve" title="Jacques Villeneuve">Jacques Villeneuve</a> failing to finish in his <a href="/wiki/Williams_FW19" title="Williams FW19">Williams</a>-Renault, Schumacher extended his lead over the Canadian in the Drivers' Championship to 10 points.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Report">Report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1997_German_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1997_German_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Going into the race, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_World_Drivers%27_Champions" title="List of Formula One World Drivers&#39; Champions">Drivers' Championship</a> had developed into a battle between <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Ferrari</a> driver <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schumacher" title="Michael Schumacher">Michael Schumacher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Williams_Grand_Prix_Engineering" title="Williams Grand Prix Engineering">Williams</a> driver <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Villeneuve" title="Jacques Villeneuve">Jacques Villeneuve</a>. Schumacher led Villeneuve by four points, 47 to 43, although Villeneuve had won four races to Schumacher's three. <a href="/wiki/Jean_Alesi" title="Jean Alesi">Jean Alesi</a>, driving for <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Formula" title="Benetton Formula">Benetton</a>, was a distant third on 21 points.<sup id="cite_ref-points_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-points-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_World_Constructors%27_Champions" title="List of Formula One World Constructors&#39; Champions">Constructors' Championship</a> had become a battle between Ferrari and Williams, with the Italian team leading by three points, 65 to 62, and both well clear of Benetton in third on 35 points.<sup id="cite_ref-points_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-points-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/1997_British_Grand_Prix" title="1997 British Grand Prix">British Grand Prix</a> on 13 July, the teams conducted testing sessions at the <a href="/wiki/Autodromo_Nazionale_Monza" class="mw-redirect" title="Autodromo Nazionale Monza">Autodromo Nazionale Monza</a> from July 14–17.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shinji_Nakano" title="Shinji Nakano">Shinji Nakano</a> (Prost) set the fastest time on the first day, while <a href="/wiki/Giancarlo_Fisichella" title="Giancarlo Fisichella">Giancarlo Fisichella</a> (Jordan) topped the second day's running. <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Herbert" title="Johnny Herbert">Johnny Herbert</a> (Sauber) was fastest on the third day and <a href="/wiki/Ralf_Schumacher" title="Ralf Schumacher">Ralf Schumacher</a> (Jordan) set the fastest time on the final day of running.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There was one driver change heading into the race. Having been in one of the two Benetton cars since the seventh race of the season at <a href="/wiki/1997_Canadian_Grand_Prix" title="1997 Canadian Grand Prix">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Wurz" title="Alexander Wurz">Alexander Wurz</a> stood down from his role as race driver and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Berger" title="Gerhard Berger">Gerhard Berger</a>. Berger was forced to miss the previous three rounds due to a reoccurring <a href="/wiki/Paranasal_sinuses" title="Paranasal sinuses">sinus</a> problem, requiring two operations.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 22 July, four days before the event's first free practice sessions took place, Benetton confirmed the team would sign Fisichella for 1998, while the organisers of the German Grand Prix signed a deal with the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile" title="Fédération Internationale de l&#39;Automobile">Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile</a> (FIA), to continue hosting the race until 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Practice_and_qualifying">Practice and qualifying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1997_German_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Practice and qualifying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Four practice sessions were held before the Sunday race—two on Friday, and two on Saturday. The Friday morning and afternoon sessions each lasted an hour. The third and final practice sessions were held on Saturday morning and lasted 45 minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-pg220_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pg220-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The two practice sessions were affected by occasionally damp and wet conditions, which made the track moderately slippery. Ralf Schumacher set the session's fastest time, with a lap of 1:46.196, one-tenth of a second quicker than Michael Schumacher.<sup id="cite_ref-germany97_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-germany97-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Saturday's afternoon qualifying session lasted for an hour. Each driver was limited to twelve laps, with the grid order decided by the drivers' fastest laps. During this session, the <a href="/wiki/107%25_rule" title="107% rule">107% rule</a> was in effect, which necessitated each driver set a time within 107% of the quickest lap to qualify for the race.<sup id="cite_ref-pg220_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pg220-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Berger clinched the twelfth pole position of his career, and the first for the Benetton team since the <a href="/wiki/1995_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1995 Japanese Grand Prix">1995 Japanese Grand Prix</a> with a time of 1:41.873. He said his lap time was "more or less" capable of what he could achieve and almost lost control of his Benetton on his final timed run before he spun at Sachs Kurve. Berger was joined on the front row of the <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_motorsport_terms#G" title="Glossary of motorsport terms">grid</a> by Fisichella who recorded a lap 0.023 seconds slower than Berger and felt he could have taken pole-position as he slid at Opel corner. Häkkinen qualified third and was satisfied with his performance; he was quicker than Berger in the first sector of the track but drove on at the Senna chicane and was delayed by Berger's spin. Michael Schumacher took fourth and experimented with a higher downforce set-up during the session. He was ahead of Frentzen in the faster of the two Williams and was afflicted with poor grip and balance. Alesi took sixth having spun his car in the final minute of the session. Ralf Schumacher braked late for the Ost chicane on his first timed run, and lost four-tenths of a second in the track's infield section during his next run and secured seventh overall. Coulthard managed eighth and spun off into the <a href="/wiki/Run-off_area" title="Run-off area">gravel trap</a> at the turn 15 right-hand corner towards the end of the session. Villeneuve was afflicted with a 3 kilometres per hour (1.9&#160;mph) straight-line speed deficit in his race car and switched to the spare Williams set up for Frentzen which was harder to handle and he was restricted to ninth. Irvine completed the top ten and reported his car ran badly over the kerbs lining the track but was confident he would have a more competitive Grand Prix.<sup id="cite_ref-fiasaturday_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fiasaturday-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gpcomdeu_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gpcomdeu-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Race">Race</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1997_German_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Race">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The drivers took to the track at 09:30 <a href="/wiki/Central_European_Summer_Time" title="Central European Summer Time">CEST</a> (<a href="/wiki/UTC" class="mw-redirect" title="UTC">UTC</a>+1) for a 30-minute warm-up session.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Giancarlo_Fisichella" title="Giancarlo Fisichella">Giancarlo Fisichella</a> took his first ever front-row start, and was challenging Berger for the win until he punctured a tyre on the debris of <a href="/wiki/Rubens_Barrichello" title="Rubens Barrichello">Rubens Barrichello</a>'s blown engine. This same incident had helped him gain the lead, as the smoke from the engine delayed Berger prior to his pit stop. Fisichella only led for two laps before Berger repassed him. After Fisichella broke down due to damage from the flailing tyre (caused while he tried to drive back to the pits), Michael Schumacher gave him a lift back to the pits after the race.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jacques_Villeneuve" title="Jacques Villeneuve">Jacques Villeneuve</a> had a disastrous race, spinning off while trying to overtake rookie <a href="/wiki/Jarno_Trulli" title="Jarno Trulli">Jarno Trulli</a>, thus losing championship ground to Schumacher. The latter's team-mate <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Irvine" title="Eddie Irvine">Eddie Irvine</a> and Villeneuve's team-mate <a href="/wiki/Heinz-Harald_Frentzen" title="Heinz-Harald Frentzen">Heinz-Harald Frentzen</a> collided at the first corner, with <a href="/wiki/David_Coulthard" title="David Coulthard">David Coulthard</a> also forced out by damage from the incident.
</p><p>Berger's last win would also be the last for Benetton, just as Berger's <a href="/wiki/1986_Mexican_Grand_Prix" title="1986 Mexican Grand Prix">first win</a> had been the team's first. It was also Benetton's only win as an Italian constructor. As of 2020<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=1997_German_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, this is also the last race won by an <a href="/wiki/Formula_One_drivers_from_Austria" title="Formula One drivers from Austria">Austrian driver</a>.
</p>
