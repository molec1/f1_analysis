<p>The <b>1951 French Grand Prix</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> <a href="/wiki/Motor_race" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor race">motor race</a> held at <a href="/wiki/Reims-Gueux" title="Reims-Gueux">Reims-Gueux</a> on 1 July 1951. It was race 4 of 8 in the <a href="/wiki/1951_World_Championship_of_Drivers" class="mw-redirect" title="1951 World Championship of Drivers">1951 World Championship of Drivers</a> and was won by <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel_Fangio" title="Juan Manuel Fangio">Juan Manuel Fangio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Fagioli" title="Luigi Fagioli">Luigi Fagioli</a> driving an <a href="/wiki/Alfa_Romeo_in_Formula_One" title="Alfa Romeo in Formula One">Alfa Romeo</a>. It was the first of three occasions where two drivers would be credited with a Grand Prix win after sharing a car.
</p><p>The race, which also carried the honorific title of <i><a href="/wiki/European_Grand_Prix" title="European Grand Prix">European Grand Prix</a></i>, saw the World Championship debuts of <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Gordini" title="Aldo Gordini">Aldo Gordini</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Simon_(racing_driver)" title="André Simon (racing driver)">André Simon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Onofre_Marim%C3%B3n" title="Onofre Marimón">Onofre Marimón</a>. Fagioli's victory, his first in a World Championship race, made him the oldest driver to win a World Championship Grand Prix, a record he still holds.
</p><p>This race also holds the record for the longest Formula One Grand Prix in terms of total distance needed to cover. 77 laps of the 4.856 mile Reims-Gueux circuit totaled to 373 miles.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Report">Report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1951_French_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About 10 laps into the race, the engine in Fangio's car began misfiring, so he stopped at the pits to have the magneto changed, but only completed one further lap before stopping again. Around this time, the gearbox in Ascari's Ferrari had broken, and he retired, although he took over the car of González, who had been pushing very hard. When Fagioli came in for his fuel stop, the team ordered Fagioli and Fangio to swap cars; Fagioli's car was running healthily whereas Fangio's car had all sorts of problems. Fuel stops and problems for the Ferraris enabled Fangio to make his way into the lead and win the race, with Ascari in González's original car finishing 2nd, 52 seconds behind. Fagioli, in Fangio's original car, finished 11th, 22 laps behind.<sup id="cite_ref-LangVol1P_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LangVol1P-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Fagioli, a veteran racing driver who had been racing Grand Prix cars since the 1920s and known for his fiery temperament was so furious over handing his car over to Fangio that he quit Grand Prix racing on the spot; he only raced this one championship race in 1951 and had not competed at the previous rounds at Bern and Spa.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shared_drives">Shared drives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1951_French_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shared drives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Car #8: Fagioli (first 20 laps) then Fangio (57 laps). They shared the points for the win (Fangio scored one extra point for setting the fastest race lap).</li>
