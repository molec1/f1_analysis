<p>The <b>1990 Japanese Grand Prix</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> motor race held on 21 October 1990 at <a href="/wiki/Suzuka_Circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Suzuka Circuit">Suzuka</a>. It was the fifteenth and penultimate race of the <a href="/wiki/1990_Formula_One_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1990 Formula One season">1990 Formula One season</a>. It was the 16th <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="Japanese Grand Prix">Japanese Grand Prix</a> and the 6th held at Suzuka.
</p><p>The race is best remembered for the first corner collision between World Championship rivals Brazilian driver <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a> and French driver <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a>, the second consecutive year the two had collided at this race with heavy championship repercussions. It immediately put both cars out of the race and secured for Senna his second World Championship, a reversal of fortunes from the <a href="/wiki/1989_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Japanese Grand Prix">1989 Japanese Grand Prix</a>, where the collision had secured the championship for Prost.
</p><p>The race saw a best result to that point for the <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Formula" title="Benetton Formula">Benetton Formula</a> team, with their drivers Brazilian veteran <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Piquet" title="Nelson Piquet">Nelson Piquet</a> and his protégé <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Moreno" title="Roberto Moreno">Roberto Moreno</a> finishing first and second in their <a href="/wiki/Benetton_B190" title="Benetton B190">Benetton B190s</a>. It was back to back wins for Benetton in Japan after the team's win the <a href="/wiki/1989_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Japanese Grand Prix">previous year</a>. Japanese driver <a href="/wiki/Aguri_Suzuki" title="Aguri Suzuki">Aguri Suzuki</a> scored a career best result for himself and the <a href="/wiki/Larrousse" title="Larrousse">Larrousse</a> team, finishing third in his <a href="/wiki/Lola_LC90" title="Lola LC90">Lola LC90</a> in the only podium driver and team would achieve.
</p><p>With Ferrari scoring no points after <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a>'s retirement, the <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a> team secured their sixth and third consecutive Constructors' Championship.
</p><p>As of 2020<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=1990_Japanese_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, this race marks the only time a <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini" title="Lamborghini">Lamborghini</a> powered car finished on the podium in Formula One.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-race">Pre-race</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1990_Japanese_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pre-race">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There were many announcements prior to the race, <a href="/wiki/Brabham" title="Brabham">Brabham</a> announced they would use <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motor_Company" title="Yamaha Motor Company">Yamaha</a> engines for 1991, <a href="/wiki/Footwork_Arrows" title="Footwork Arrows">Footwork</a> also announced a <a href="/wiki/Porsche_in_Formula_One" class="mw-redirect" title="Porsche in Formula One">Porsche</a> engine deal for 1991 and they also retained both drivers in <a href="/wiki/Alex_Caffi" title="Alex Caffi">Alex Caffi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michele_Alboreto" title="Michele Alboreto">Michele Alboreto</a>. Prior to the race, the <a href="/wiki/Life_Racing_Engines" title="Life Racing Engines">Life Racing Engines</a> and <a href="/wiki/EuroBrun" title="EuroBrun">EuroBrun</a> teams withdrew from the sport. EuroBrun's Roberto Moreno joined the Benetton team replacing last year's race winner <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Nannini" title="Alessandro Nannini">Alessandro Nannini</a>, whose Formula One career ended in a helicopter crash after the <a href="/wiki/1990_Spanish_Grand_Prix" title="1990 Spanish Grand Prix">Spanish Grand Prix</a>.
</p><p>Alesi did not start due to a neck injury during Friday's practice. As his grid position was left empty, this was the third consecutive race to have only 25 starters instead of the usual 26.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a> also announced a U-turn on his decision to retire by making public his agreement to join Williams-Renault for two years from 1991 after being given assurances from Frank Williams, Patrick Head and Renault that they could deliver him a car in which he could win a world championship and that he would be the team's undisputed #1 driver. On Saturday <a href="/wiki/Soichiro_Honda" title="Soichiro Honda">Soichiro Honda</a>, the founder of <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a>, met Ayrton Senna in the McLaren pit.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualifying">Qualifying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1990_Japanese_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Qualifying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Qualifying_report">Qualifying report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1990_Japanese_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Qualifying report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the withdrawal of the EuroBrun and Life teams, there was no need for a pre-qualifying session as only 30 cars remained in the event. The four drivers relieved of the necessity to pre-qualify, <a href="/wiki/Yannick_Dalmas" title="Yannick Dalmas">Yannick Dalmas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabriele_Tarquini" title="Gabriele Tarquini">Gabriele Tarquini</a> (both <a href="/wiki/Automobiles_Gonfaronnaises_Sportives" title="Automobiles Gonfaronnaises Sportives">AGS</a>), <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Grouillard" title="Olivier Grouillard">Olivier Grouillard</a> (<a href="/wiki/Osella" title="Osella">Osella</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Gachot" title="Bertrand Gachot">Bertrand Gachot</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Coloni" title="Scuderia Coloni">Coloni</a>) were ultimately the four drivers that failed to qualify for the race. Gachot crashed heavily in the Friday session. <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Moreno" title="Roberto Moreno">Roberto Moreno</a>, who had left EuroBrun and joined <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Formula" title="Benetton Formula">Benetton</a>, qualified easily in ninth position.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Race">Race</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1990_Japanese_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Race">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Race_report">Race report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1990_Japanese_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Race report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This race is best known for its first corner incident involving world championship contenders <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a>. Senna secured the pole, but was unhappy with the side of the track it was situated on, claiming that pole should always be on the racing line. He and <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Berger" title="Gerhard Berger">Gerhard Berger</a> then went to the Japanese stewards, to request a change of position of pole to the cleaner left side of the track. The stewards initially agreed but an injunction by FISA president <a href="/wiki/Jean_Marie_Balestre" class="mw-redirect" title="Jean Marie Balestre">Jean Marie Balestre</a> later that night rejected the decision and the original pole position remained on the dirtier, less grippy right side of the track. In addition, as revealed by F1 journalist, Maurice Hamilton,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the FIA had warned that crossing the yellow line of the pit exit on the right to better position oneself at the first corner would have not been appropriate, further infuriating Senna.
</p><p>Some in the Formula One paddock found Senna's complaints as strange given that the pole was actually on the same side of the track (the inside next to the pit wall) as it had been since the first Japanese Grand Prix held there in <a href="/wiki/1987_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1987 Japanese Grand Prix">1987</a>. Many also noted that Senna had not complained about the position of the pole in either <a href="/wiki/1988_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1988 Japanese Grand Prix">1988</a> or <a href="/wiki/1989_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="1989 Japanese Grand Prix">1989</a>, both races he had started on pole and both races he was fighting Prost (who qualified 2nd in both years) for the World Championship.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> 
</p><p>After this, Senna vowed that if Prost (starting second) got the advantage into the first corner, which most were sure he would, Senna would attempt to take the lead into the first corner, regardless of the consequences.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The two drivers made contact in the first corner, sending both drivers off the track and into instant retirement. The crash meant that Senna had clinched the Drivers' Championship for a second time, as with one race left in the season, Prost could not overtake his points tally. Benetton-Ford's dominance of the podium prevented Ferrari from scoring enough points to stop <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a> clinching its sixth constructors' title.
</p><p>The two discussed the event afterwards<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> with Senna claiming it was not how he wanted it but how it had to be, with many others accepting his actions as a solution (or revenge, to an extent) to the incident the year before.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Prost was infuriated by this and publicly slammed the move as "disgusting" and Senna as "a man without value". He later admitted that he almost retired from the sport instantly after the incident.
</p><p>The pair went on to win one more championship each and eventually reconciled their differences in their <a href="/wiki/1993_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1993 Australian Grand Prix">final Grand Prix</a> together.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the collision, the race proceeded with Gerhard Berger's <a href="/wiki/McLaren_MP4/5B" class="mw-redirect" title="McLaren MP4/5B">McLaren MP4/5B</a> leading and <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Mansell" title="Nigel Mansell">Nigel Mansell</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ferrari_641" title="Ferrari 641">Ferrari 641</a> second.  Berger spun off at the first corner on lap 2, on sand thrown onto the track by the Senna/Prost collision, leaving Mansell to lead the race from the two Benettons of Piquet and Moreno. Anticipating that Benetton would follow their usual strategy of not making a pit stop, Mansell built up a gap until he pitted for tyres at the end of lap 26. After a quick stop, he left his box with heavy wheelspin, and a <a href="/wiki/Driveshaft" class="mw-redirect" title="Driveshaft">driveshaft</a> failed.  The Ferrari pulled over at the end of the pit lane and retired. Piquet inherited the lead and retained it all the way to the chequered flag, with his teammate Moreno following closely. <a href="/wiki/Aguri_Suzuki" title="Aguri Suzuki">Aguri Suzuki</a> also drove a non-stop race, finishing third, the first Japanese driver to do so. The two <a href="/wiki/Williams_FW13B" class="mw-redirect" title="Williams FW13B">Williams FW13B</a>-Renaults of <a href="/wiki/Riccardo_Patrese" title="Riccardo Patrese">Riccardo Patrese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thierry_Boutsen" title="Thierry Boutsen">Thierry Boutsen</a> finished fourth and fifth, while <a href="/wiki/Satoru_Nakajima" title="Satoru Nakajima">Satoru Nakajima</a> finished sixth in a <a href="/wiki/Tyrrell_019" title="Tyrrell 019">Tyrrell 019</a>, the second Japanese driver in the points.
</p><p>As of September&#160;2020<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=1990_Japanese_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, this was the last race where no European driver finished the race on the podium; two South American drivers and an Asian driver filled the three podium places for this race. It was also the only race where the <a href="/wiki/Larrousse" title="Larrousse">Larrousse</a> team scored a podium finish, during their eight seasons of competing in Formula One. It was also the first and only podium finish for the <a href="/wiki/Lamborghini_V12_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Lamborghini V12 engine">Lamborghini V12 engine</a> in Formula One. Moreover, it was also the last of Brazil's eleven one-twos in Formula One, the only one featuring Piquet and Moreno – of the other ten, eight featured Piquet and Senna and the other two, <a href="/wiki/Emerson_Fittipaldi" title="Emerson Fittipaldi">Emerson Fittipaldi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Carlos_Pace" class="mw-redirect" title="José Carlos Pace">José Carlos Pace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>. <a href="/wiki/Aguri_Suzuki" title="Aguri Suzuki">Aguri Suzuki</a>'s podium finish was the first for a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One_drivers_from_Japan" title="Formula One drivers from Japan">Japanese driver</a> (later matched by <a href="/wiki/Takuma_Sato" title="Takuma Sato">Takuma Sato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kamui_Kobayashi" title="Kamui Kobayashi">Kamui Kobayashi</a>) and the last for a Japanese driver at his home race until <a href="/wiki/Kamui_Kobayashi" title="Kamui Kobayashi">Kamui Kobayashi</a> duplicated the feat at the <a href="/wiki/2012_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="2012 Japanese Grand Prix">2012 Japanese Grand Prix</a>.
</p>
