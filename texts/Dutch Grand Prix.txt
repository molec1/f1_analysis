<p>The <b>Dutch Grand Prix</b> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Grote Prijs van Nederland</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> automobile race held at <a href="/wiki/Circuit_Zandvoort" title="Circuit Zandvoort">Circuit Zandvoort</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Holland" title="North Holland">North Holland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, from 1948 to 1985 and scheduled to be held from <a href="/wiki/2021_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="2021 Formula One World Championship">2021</a> onwards. It was a part of the World Championship from 1952, and designated the <a href="/wiki/European_Grand_Prix" title="European Grand Prix">European Grand Prix</a> twice, 1962 and 1976, when this title was an honorary designation given each year to one Grand Prix race in Europe.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_circuit">Original circuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Original circuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town of <a href="/wiki/Zandvoort" title="Zandvoort">Zandvoort</a> is situated in the dunes of North Holland's <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> coastline half an hour west from the Dutch capital of <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, and the circuit itself is located right next to the beach. There were minor races on a street circuit in the town in the 1930s but it was the German invasion of the Netherlands which proved to be a stroke of luck for the locals. In an effort to stop his townsfolk being sent to Germany to work, legend has it that the Mayor of Zandvoort convinced the Germans to allow them to construct a straight road through the dunes down which the Germans could hold impressive parades once victory had been achieved. This was later linked to other roads which were used to access coastal defence positions.
</p><p>After the war some of these roads were widened and linked together and a racing circuit was designed, not as legend has it by <a href="/wiki/John_Hugenholtz" title="John Hugenholtz">John Hugenholtz</a>, but rather by a group of officials from the Royal Dutch Motorcycle Association, with advice from <a href="/wiki/Bentley_Boys" title="Bentley Boys">Bentley Boy</a> <a href="/wiki/S._C._H._%22Sammy%22_Davis" class="mw-redirect" title="S. C. H. &quot;Sammy&quot; Davis">Sammy Davis</a>, who had won the <a href="/wiki/1927_24_Hours_of_Le_Mans" title="1927 24 Hours of Le Mans">Le Mans 24 Hours in 1927</a>. The first race took place in 1948, under the title of the Zandvoort Grand Prix. It was won by Thailand's <a href="/wiki/Prince_Bira" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Bira">Prince Bira</a> in an old Maserati.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The race was then won by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Rosier" title="Louis Rosier">Louis Rosier</a> in 1950 and 1951. 1952 was the year the Dutch Grand Prix was part of the <a href="/wiki/1952_Formula_One_season" title="1952 Formula One season">third Formula One World Championship</a>; this and the next year's races were won by Italian <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Ascari" title="Alberto Ascari">Alberto Ascari</a>. The race was not held in 1954 due to a lack of money to hold the race,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and 1955 saw yet another demonstration of <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_in_Formula_One" title="Mercedes-Benz in Formula One">Mercedes-Benz</a>'s dominance, with Argentine <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel_Fangio" title="Juan Manuel Fangio">Juan Manuel Fangio</a> and Briton <a href="/wiki/Stirling_Moss" title="Stirling Moss">Stirling Moss</a> dominating the proceedings; Moss followed Fangio closely all the way. The 1956 and 1957 races were cancelled because of apparent lack of money, which was indirectly caused by the 1956–1957 <a href="/wiki/Suez_Crisis" title="Suez Crisis">Suez Crisis</a>. The 1958 Dutch Grand Prix was won by Moss in a <a href="/wiki/Vanwall" title="Vanwall">Vanwall</a>. 1959 saw Swede <a href="/wiki/Jo_Bonnier" title="Jo Bonnier">Jo Bonnier</a> win his only Formula One championship event and 1960 saw <a href="/wiki/Dan_Gurney" title="Dan Gurney">Dan Gurney</a> have an accident and a spectator was killed; the race was won by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Brabham" title="Jack Brabham">Jack Brabham</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Car_Company" title="Cooper Car Company">Cooper</a>.
</p><p>From 1963 to 1965 saw Briton <a href="/wiki/Jim_Clark" title="Jim Clark">Jim Clark</a> win all three events, and 1967 saw the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Lotus_49" title="Lotus 49">Lotus 49</a> with its brand new Ford-<a href="/wiki/Cosworth_DFV" title="Cosworth DFV">Cosworth DFV</a> engine. The DFV won on its debut with Clark driving; this engine became the most successful and widely used engine among private teams until 1985. The 1970 Championship, however, saw the 49's successor, the <a href="/wiki/Lotus_72" title="Lotus 72">72</a> (which was just as advanced a design as the 49 had been 3 years earlier) win comprehensively with <a href="/wiki/Jochen_Rindt" title="Jochen Rindt">Jochen Rindt</a> behind the wheel.
</p><p>Tragedy struck, when Briton <a href="/wiki/Piers_Courage" title="Piers Courage">Piers Courage</a>, driving a <a href="/wiki/Frank_Williams_(Formula_One)" title="Frank Williams (Formula One)">Frank Williams</a>, crashed heavily near the notoriously fast Tunnel Oost corner after a wheel came off and hit him on the head, which killed him. The car, with Courage still in it, then caught fire and burned to the ground. The 1971 Championship saw <a href="/wiki/Jacky_Ickx" title="Jacky Ickx">Jacky Ickx</a> win in a <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Ferrari</a> after a spirited battle with Mexican <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Rodr%C3%ADguez_(racing_driver)" title="Pedro Rodríguez (racing driver)">Pedro Rodriguez</a> in a <a href="/wiki/British_Racing_Motors" title="British Racing Motors">BRM</a> in rain-soaked conditions. There was no 1972 race. It was originally on that year's calendar, but the drivers refused to race at Zandvoort, because the facilities and conditions of the circuit were out-of-date with Grand Prix racing at that time.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redeveloped_circuit">Redeveloped circuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Redeveloped circuit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Zandvoort had been extensively modified during its absence from the Grand Prix calendar. It had been lined with <a href="/wiki/Traffic_barrier" title="Traffic barrier">Armco</a> and the cars were protected from the sand dunes and track-side obstacles. New pits were built, and the circuit also saw a chicane placed before Bosuit, the very high-speed corner that went into the pit-straight. For the 1973 race, in an indirect celebration of the efforts put forth, there was a special atmosphere at that weekend and everyone was happy, especially the organizers. But in a cruel twist of fate, that race was to be yet another black mark on Zandvoort's history and reputation. In a race that was thought to be one of the most well organized Grands Prix yet seen, it was actually disorganization and a total lack of clear communication that would be ultimately responsible for what was to happen. On the eighth lap of the race, Briton <a href="/wiki/Roger_Williamson" title="Roger Williamson">Roger Williamson</a> (in only his second ever Formula One race) crashed heavily near Tunnel Oost and his car caught fire while scraping along the tarmac. Williamson was uninjured during the crash; but time was running out; he could not free himself from the car. Williamson's countryman <a href="/wiki/David_Purley" title="David Purley">David Purley</a> stopped alongside, crossed the track and ran over to the burning March. Purley tried in vain to turn the car upright. There appeared to have been ample time to right the car and pull Williamson out, but as desperately as he tried, Purley was unable to do it by himself, and the marshals, who were not wearing <a href="/wiki/Flame_retardant" title="Flame retardant">flame retardant</a> <a href="/wiki/Overalls" title="Overalls">overalls</a>, were unable and unwilling to help due to the intense heat. Race control assumed that it was Purley's car that had crashed and that the driver had escaped unharmed. Many drivers who saw Purley waving them down to stop assumed that he was trying to put a fire out from his own car, having safely exited it, and thus did not know that a second driver had been involved. As a result, the race continued at full pace while Purley desperately tried to save the life of Williamson. Due to a group of race officials standing around Williamson's burning car doing absolutely nothing to help and even hindering the situation (by throwing away the fire extinguisher Purley was using over the Armco and down a slope), this did not work, and Williamson died not of skin burns but of asphyxiation. Purley was later awarded the <a href="/wiki/George_Medal" title="George Medal">George Medal</a> for his actions. The race was won by Tyrrell driver <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Stewart" title="Jackie Stewart">Jackie Stewart</a> (who broke Jim Clark's record for the most career Grand Prix victories that weekend) and his teammate <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Cevert" title="François Cevert">François Cevert</a> finished 2nd; but no one felt like celebrating; it was one of the darkest moments in the history of the sport.
</p><p>1974 saw the re-emerging Ferrari team dominate with Austrian <a href="/wiki/Niki_Lauda" title="Niki Lauda">Niki Lauda</a> winning; and 1975 saw Briton <a href="/wiki/James_Hunt" title="James Hunt">James Hunt</a> win his first championship Formula One race in his <a href="/wiki/Hesketh_Racing" title="Hesketh Racing">Hesketh</a>. 1976 saw Hunt win again while Lauda was recovering from his dreadful crash at the Nürburgring. 1977 was probably remembered for an incident between Hunt and American <a href="/wiki/Mario_Andretti" title="Mario Andretti">Mario Andretti</a>. Andretti attempted ambitiously to pass Hunt at the 180-degree Tarzan corner; the two cars touched and both were out of the race. Andretti won the 1978 running; his last Formula One victory. 1979 saw a change to the circuit to slow cars coming into Tunnel Oost; there was a high-speed temporary chicane put there. Canadian <a href="/wiki/Gilles_Villeneuve" title="Gilles Villeneuve">Gilles Villeneuve</a> had crashed there while battling ferociously with Australian <a href="/wiki/Alan_Jones_(racing_driver)" title="Alan Jones (racing driver)">Alan Jones</a> and damaged his left-rear suspension. But he carried on; but on the start of the next lap he went off again at Tarzan. Refusing to give up, Villeneuve, to the shock of many, went into reverse gear and drove his Ferrari out of the muddy run-off area and back onto the circuit. About halfway distance, the car's left rear rim and wheel with the suspension totally shattered was being dragged by the car as it went along; which made the Ferrari nearly impossible to drive. Villeneuve, displaying his now legendary car control, made it back to the pits without crashing or going off and retired from the race; the Grand Prix was won by Jones. 1980 saw the chicane removed and was replaced by a slower chicane before Tunnel Oost. 1981 saw a big battle between Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Renault_F1" class="mw-redirect" title="Renault F1">Renault</a> and Jones in a <a href="/wiki/WilliamsF1" class="mw-redirect" title="WilliamsF1">Williams</a>; Prost came out on top to win. The 1982 event was won by Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Didier_Pironi" title="Didier Pironi">Didier Pironi</a> in a Ferrari; his countryman <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Arnoux" title="René Arnoux">René Arnoux</a> had a dreadful crash at the end of the pit straight going into Tarzan; his front suspension failed on his ground-effect Renault and he went head on into the barriers; fortunately he was uninjured. 1983 saw a battle between championship contenders Prost and Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Piquet" title="Nelson Piquet">Nelson Piquet</a>. Prost attempted to pass Piquet at Tarzan but the Frenchman punted Piquet off and Prost crashed soon afterwards. 1985 saw Lauda take his 25th and final Grand Prix victory while holding off his charging <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a> teammate Prost near the end of the race.
</p><p>1985 was the race's final running, as the company that ran the circuit (CENAV) went out of business, marking the end of the old Zandvoort circuit. The track, owned by the municipality of Zandvoort, was not used for some time and part of the grounds and approximately half of the track was sold in 1987 to Vendorado, a bungalow park developer at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The track was eventually redesigned and is still used for other disciplines of motorsport.
</p>
<h3><span id="2021.E2.80.93"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2021–">2021–</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2021–">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 14 May 2019, the Dutch Grand Prix at the Zandvoort track was announced for the <a href="/wiki/2020_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="2020 Formula One World Championship">2020</a> Formula One calendar.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In March 2020 the return of event was postponed in response to the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic" title="COVID-19 pandemic">COVID-19 pandemic</a>; it was later cancelled altogether with the event's return now due in <a href="/wiki/2021_Formula_One_World_Championship" title="2021 Formula One World Championship">2021</a>.
</p>
