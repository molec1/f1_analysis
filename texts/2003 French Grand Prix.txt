<p>The <b>2003 French Grand Prix</b> (formally the <b>LXXXIX Mobil 1 Grand Prix de France</b>) was a <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> motor race held on 6 July 2003 at the <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_Nevers_Magny-Cours" title="Circuit de Nevers Magny-Cours">Circuit de Nevers Magny-Cours</a>. It was the tenth race of the <a href="/wiki/2003_Formula_One_season" class="mw-redirect" title="2003 Formula One season">2003 Formula One season</a>. The 70-lap race was won by <a href="/wiki/Ralf_Schumacher" title="Ralf Schumacher">Ralf Schumacher</a> driving for the <a href="/wiki/Williams_Grand_Prix_Engineering" title="Williams Grand Prix Engineering">Williams</a> team after starting from <a href="/wiki/Pole_position" title="Pole position">pole position</a>. <a href="/wiki/Juan_Pablo_Montoya" title="Juan Pablo Montoya">Juan Pablo Montoya</a> finished second in the other Williams car, with <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schumacher" title="Michael Schumacher">Michael Schumacher</a> third driving for <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Ferrari</a>. Ralf Schumacher's victory was his second consecutive win of the season having won the preceding <a href="/wiki/2003_European_Grand_Prix" title="2003 European Grand Prix">European Grand Prix</a> at the <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrburgring" title="Nürburgring">Nürburgring</a>.
</p><p>As a consequence of the race, Michael Schumacher extended his lead in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_World_Drivers%27_Champions" title="List of Formula One World Drivers&#39; Champions">World Drivers' Championship</a> to eight points over <a href="/wiki/Kimi_R%C3%A4ikk%C3%B6nen" title="Kimi Räikkönen">Kimi Räikkönen</a>, with Ralf Schumacher a further three points behind. In the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Formula_One_World_Constructors%27_Champions" title="List of Formula One World Constructors&#39; Champions">World Constructors' Championship</a>, Williams reduced the gap to Ferrari from thirteen points to three points.
</p><p>This was the first French Grand Prix held at the upgraded version of <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_Nevers_Magny-Cours" title="Circuit de Nevers Magny-Cours">Magny-Cours</a>, having made Château d'Eau a sharper right hand corner, and a complete modification of the Lycée section, becoming a very sharp right hand turn after the back straight which then leads to a difficult final chicane next to the pit entrance. The pit lane was also significantly shortened as a result of the upgrades.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2003_French_Grand_Prix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>This race was <a href="/wiki/Ralf_Schumacher" title="Ralf Schumacher">Ralf Schumacher</a>'s 6th and final victory of his Formula One career and the only time he won from pole position.</li>
<li>As of 2020, this is the last race in which Williams achieved a 1–2 finish.</li>
<li>This race ended <a href="/wiki/David_Coulthard" title="David Coulthard">David Coulthard</a>'s 5 consecutive fastest laps in France, which began in the <a href="/wiki/1998_French_Grand_Prix" title="1998 French Grand Prix">1998 race</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jos_Verstappen" title="Jos Verstappen">Jos Verstappen</a> ended up quickest after the first qualifying session due to a drying track. This was the only time a Minardi topped the timesheets at a race weekend.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Justin_Wilson_(racing_driver)" title="Justin Wilson (racing driver)">Justin Wilson</a>'s time after Q1 was deleted after scrutineering found his car was 2&#160;kg underweight. It was 1:20.968 which at the time was good enough for 2nd place giving Minardi a 1–2.</li></ul>
